01 What This Is & Why It's Different

Who EduFin is: EduFin is our client, a lending business currently facing a portfolio crisis. Their board knows something is wrong and has asked us to investigate and quantify it.

Think of this document as your first day on the job. You are the Analyst. I am your Lead. Together, we have to respond to EduFin with our findings by Friday, 7 PM. This guide is your instruction set for how we work through that engagement, day by day.

This is a 5-day business investigation. You are not here to watch a tutorial or copy answers. You are here to investigate a real business problem, make judgment calls, write queries, test assumptions, and explain what your analysis actually means.

The goal is reasoning, judgment, and decision direction, not output volume.

You will move through three investigation cases in this sequence:

Why this feels different from normal SQL practice

REASON 01

No single right answer

Two people can query the same data and reach different conclusions if their assumptions differ. What matters is the quality of your reasoning, not whether your output matches someone else's.

REASON 02

Ambiguity is intentional

Some parts of this investigation will not have one clean answer. That is by design. Judgment is the skill being tested, not matching query output.

REASON 03

AI is allowed. Unexplained work is not.

Use any AI tool you want. Every query you submit must be explainable: what it checks, why you wrote it, what you concluded. A query that runs is not automatically a correct answer.

The distinction: Most SQL practice uses clean data with one right answer. This investigation puts you inside a real business problem and requires you to move, without perfect information.

Writing SQL is not the test here. Using it to understand what's actually happening in the business is.

05 How to Work, Submit & Get Unstuck

How to work well

Do not open the case and start writing queries immediately. Work through the problem in this order.

1
Read the question carefully.
2
Identify what the business is really asking, not just what the query requires.
3
Decide which data points matter for this decision.
4
Write the query.
5
Check whether the result actually answers the business question, not just whether the query ran.
6
Note what changed in your thinking after seeing the result.
↑
Do not optimize for number of queries. Optimize for quality of reasoning.
Write your analysis for the decision-maker, not for the query editor. The person reading your submission is checking for judgment, not syntax.

What to submit

Every submission must include:

  • Your SQL queries
  • Your reasoning: why you wrote the query the way you did
  • What you expected before running it, and what you actually found
  • What is still unclear or unresolved
  • Your conclusion: what decision direction your analysis points to
Submit through the official form only.
The submission form link is in Section 04 (Where Everything Is). It's shared with you directly, not located inside Graphy.
WhatsApp messages are not submissions. Screenshots without reasoning are not submissions. Work outside the form will not be counted.

If you get stuck

Follow this order before asking for help:

  • 01
    Check the Kick-Off Guide again. The answer is often already here
  • 02
    Check the edufin-faq page: skillaipath.com/edufin-faq
  • 03
    Check Databricks Setup assets. Most environment errors are covered there
  • 04
    Re-read the case file from the beginning
  • 05
    Post in the Graphy Community with full context (see below)

How to ask for help

Every request must include:

  • Which case you're working on
  • Which query or step you are working on
  • What you tried
  • What result or error came up
  • What exactly is unclear
Do not post "it's not working" without context. A message without these details cannot be answered usefully.

06 Rules and Certificate Consideration

Allowed

AI tools

Use any AI tool. If you submit a query, you must be able to explain it. If you cannot, the submission does not count.

Allowed

Rework and resubmission

You may rework and resubmit any case. A stronger second submission replaces a weaker first one.

Allowed

Discussion

You may discuss approach and reasoning. You may not submit work that is not yours or that you cannot explain.

Not accepted

Output without reasoning

Work you cannot explain does not count. Disappearing without context during the investigation window is noted.

Counts toward certificate consideration

  • Required submissions completed, with reasoning, following the case sequence
  • Active, consistent participation through the investigation window
  • Attendance on Friday review and presentation call

Weakens certificate consideration

  • Disappearing without context, or skipping the case sequence
  • Output you can't explain, or work with no reasoning
  • Missing the Friday call without prior context or notice
This is not a course completion badge. It reflects whether you worked through a real investigation with seriousness, and whether you can show the work was yours.

08 Sharing Your Work, NDA-Safe

One rule covers all external sharing: share the lesson, not the data. Share your thinking, not the case.

Safe to share
  • Your reasoning process and what shifted during the investigation
  • What you expected versus what you found
  • One safe lesson: scale, joins, business logic, interpretation
  • A workflow snapshot without private identifiers or sensitive numbers
Do not share
  • Raw row-level data or private identifiers
  • Sensitive business numbers from the dataset
  • Full notebook exports or full case answers
  • Internal case materials
  • Public GitHub repos with case files or solution notebooks
You may name this as the EduFin Investigation. You may not share internal numbers, raw data, case files, full answers, or notebook exports.

LinkedIn

A safe post communicates the experience and one concrete lesson, nothing else.

Sample Post
Finished a 5-day SQL investigation through the EduFin Investigation, focused on portfolio health, customer risk, and time-based analysis.

What changed my thinking most was Day 2. Once the dataset scale increased, assumptions that looked stable on Day 1 had to be checked again. Some joins and result patterns behaved differently at the new scale, which forced me to revisit conclusions I had already started trusting.

Big lesson from this experience: a query running successfully is not the same as the analysis being decision-ready.

That is the real difference between practicing SQL and using SQL to investigate a business problem.
Notice what is absent: no data values, no internal client details, no case answers. The post communicates the experience, a thinking shift, and one concrete lesson, nothing else.
